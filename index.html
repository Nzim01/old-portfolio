1000 sweaters in your pocket.

A Digital Closet

Picture this: You are a high school student about to embark on a semester long study abroad program in the north of France. You will be there during the winter and therefore it is essential that you pack warm clothing. However, there is one problem, you attend boarding school and will only have two days at home to pack. Best outcome: you find all the necessary clothing you need and bring it along. Most likely outcome: You realize 5 minutes before your flight is about to depart that you have a new sweater that your aunt from New York bought for you, that you haven’t worn yet. Now you’re kicking yourself, because you realize that you will probably have to buy a new sweater in France - one that you hadn’t budgeted for. But how could you have remembered about that one sweater from months ago? It’s not as if you wear them often.

If only there was a way to keep track of all your existing clothing items without having to scour through your entire closet..

Overview

Goal: Develop an application that allows users to easily sort through and manage their closet.

Type: UI Designer

Team: Myself.

Duration: January 2016 (5 Days).

Skills: User Interface Design, Visual Design, Prototyping, Product Thinking.

Meaningful technology that connects people.

The story of David...

In 2017, David Cameron visited the campus of Brown University for a lecture. Tickets were free and my friend and I eagerly wanted to attend. This is a story of how our frustration in being unable to see Mr. Cameron, led to the founding of a startup.

Overview

Goal: Design the Ubica mobile app for students.

Type: Co-Founder of Ubica - Product Design.

Team: Samuel Aguilar (Co-Founder).

Duration: February 2017 - May 2017 (4 Months).

Skills: Interaction Design, Visual Design, Prototyping, Product Thinking.

My Role

I was part of the two-person design team, leading the user research and conducting interviews with stakeholders. I also worked alongside my partner, Sam Aguilar, in producing design iterations of the final product.

The Challenge

We approached the problem facing 3 central pain points. Apathy, Attention Span and Social Engagement. Through our research we had determined that our end-user was apathetic, had a low attention span and desired social engagement. With this setting we had to not only focus on the functionality and usability of the product - beyond current competitors - but also aim to address the aforementioned user pain points.

Insights

Social Media
There are several digital avenues used to generate a complete image of what’s happening on campus. Events were discovered primarily through email as not very many organizations were on Facebook - even fewer using Twitter. GroupMe was highly popular amongst a handful of organizations. Slack was used almost solely to coordinate internal meetings. (e.g. Ivy Film Festival).

Bulletin Boards
Found scattered across campus. Major problem was that past events did not always get taken down until days or in rare cases weeks later. They were far less reliable not as you would only know about events if you happened to walk passed a very specific board.

Word of Mouth
Friends will either call, text, or occasionally schedule events and meetings through email. To stay informed about social gatherings, most people rely on word of mouth through close friend circles.
